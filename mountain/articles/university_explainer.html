How Our AI Work Actually Works

What you'll build in this workshop, how it changes your morning, and why a person who starts every day knowing exactly what matters is the best thing that ever happened to the people they serve.

This workshop is not about AI tools. It's about you. Your knowledge, your work, your judgment, and what happens when you stop losing all of that to inbox noise and tool sprawl and whatever's screaming loudest at 9 AM.

You'll leave today with two things. A personal knowledge vault on your own machine, in plain text files you own. And a /today script that reads your vault every morning and tells you what actually matters. Not what's urgent. What matters, for the students you serve and the work only you can do.

A person who starts every day with that kind of clarity serves better. When enough people at Maryville work that way, the learners feel it. The community feels it. We didn't build a system. We built a habit.

These five layers explain how it works, from what you build today to the data strategy that gives Maryville options it's never had.

What you build today — and who it serves
Layer 1 What you build in the workshop
A vault. Your knowledge, your machine, your rules.
You build it today. You'll actually use it tomorrow.

First thing we do is set up a personal knowledge base on your computer. Your machine, plain text files, you own them. We connect it to a private repository so it syncs securely, but the originals never leave your hands.

Most people's expertise lives nowhere. It's in their head, in their inbox, scattered across tools they don't control. The vault changes that. Your meeting notes, your research, the things you've learned about the students you work with, all organized and accessible to an AI pointed at your context instead of the internet's.

When the AI helps you think through something, it's drawing on what you know, not a generic model of what everyone knows. And it stays yours.

Why it matters for our learners A person who can actually find what they know makes better decisions for the students sitting across from them. The vault makes everything else in this workshop possible.
→ enables
Layer 2 What your vault makes possible
The /today script. Your morning changes.
The vault is the foundation. This is the payoff.

Once your vault exists, we build the thing that makes it matter every day: a /today script. You run it in the morning. It reads your vault, your notes, the things you've been tracking, and tells you what deserves your attention. Not what's in your inbox. What actually matters, based on what you know.

It might surface a student who's gone quiet. It might remind you that the curriculum revision you've been noodling on connects to something you wrote down three weeks ago. Or it just says: here are the three things that will make today count. Built on your knowledge, not someone else's algorithm.

That's a person who walks into every interaction a little more prepared, a little more likely to catch the thing that would've slipped through. The people you serve feel the difference, even if they can't name it.

Why it matters for our learners A student doesn't know you ran /today that morning. They just know you remembered. You followed up. You saw the pattern. That kind of attention compounds across every person a student interacts with.
→ enables
Layer 3 What happens when many people work this way
Prepared people start connecting dots nobody asked them to connect
Nobody built a centralized brain. The people closest to the work just got equipped to act on what they already know.

When one person runs /today, they serve their students better. When fifty people do it, advisors, faculty, program directors, financial aid, something else starts to happen. Prepared people start having better conversations.

An advisor notices a pattern and mentions it to a program director who's been thinking the same thing from a different angle. A faculty member's curriculum notes connect to an employer need that someone in career services has been hearing about for months. These connections were always there. People just finally have the headspace to make them.

None of this requires a platform or a mandate. It requires people who aren't drowning, whose mornings start with clarity, who have the bandwidth to notice what they'd normally miss.

Why it matters for our learners A student's experience improves when the people around them are coordinated. Not by software. By shared awareness. When your advisor and your professor and your financial aid counselor are all a little more dialed in, you feel it. Even if nobody told them to talk to each other.
→ enables
Layer 4 What this gives the institution
Your knowledge becomes a data strategy. The university gets options it never had.
Right now, your institutional knowledge lives inside vendor databases. That's not a strategy. That's a lease.

Here's what most universities don't talk about: the reason you can't leave a SaaS platform isn't the software. It's that your data, your people's knowledge, your workflows, your institutional memory, is locked inside it. The vendor didn't steal it. You just never had anywhere else to put it. So when the contract comes up for renewal, the negotiation is already over.

The vaults change that equation. When your people's expertise lives in infrastructure you own, in plain text on your machines, you start building an institutional data layer. Not a data lake nobody uses. A living layer of knowledge that your people actually touch every morning. Once that layer exists, vendor conversations feel different.

You stop asking "can we migrate off this?" and start asking "what exactly are we paying this vendor to do that we can't do ourselves?" Sometimes the answer is "a lot" and you keep the tool. Sometimes the answer surprises you. Either way, you're asking from a position of strength because the knowledge isn't trapped anymore.

Why it matters for Maryville Every dollar spent on a SaaS license you can't walk away from is a dollar that isn't a choice. An institutional data layer doesn't mean abandoning vendors. It means every renewal is a decision, not a dependency.
→ enables
Layer 5 Why all of it matters
The dimmer switch. Dial vendors up or down because you own the foundation.
A university that owns its knowledge layer doesn't rip and replace. It adjusts.

Think of every SaaS tool you use as a light on a dimmer switch. Right now, most of those dimmers are stuck at full brightness. Not because the tool is irreplaceable, but because your data is inside it and you have no alternative. The vault, the /today scripts, the institutional data layer underneath them: that's what unsticks the switch.

Once your knowledge lives on infrastructure you control, you can make real decisions. Keep a CRM but pull the reporting into your own layer. Renew the LMS but stop paying for the analytics module you can build better internally. Let a contract lapse because the workflow already moved to something your people built in their vaults. This isn't rip-and-replace. It's the ability to dial each vendor up or down based on what they're actually worth, renegotiated from a position where walking away is real.

That's the long game. A vault is a small thing. A /today script is a morning habit. Multiply that across the people in this room and you're looking at something almost no university has: the freedom to choose its tools instead of being chosen by them. It starts this afternoon.

Why it matters for Maryville The universities that thrive in the next decade won't be the ones with the best vendor stack. They'll be the ones that own their knowledge layer and can license, replace, or build whatever serves their people best. This workshop is where that starts.
