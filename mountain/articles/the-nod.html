The Nod

What five sisters taught me about the real work of AI.

I sat in a board meeting this week at the university I serve. Around the table were administrators, faculty, finance people, and five Sisters of the Sacred Heart. Each of them has given a lifetime to education. Each of them loves this institution with a depth that most people in tech will never understand about anything. And I was about to present my AI work to all of them.

Here's what I knew walking in: if I lost the sisters, I lost the room. Not because they hold the most votes or control the budget, but because they hold the moral center. When they understand something and believe in it, everyone else feels it. And when they don't, everyone feels that too.

So I did something that most people in technology never think to do. I translated.

The Language of the Sacred Heart

AI has its own language. Neural networks, transformers, large language models, generative pre-trained architectures. The vocabulary is dense, abstract, and self-referential. If you already live in that world, the words feel natural. If you don't, they feel like a wall.

The Sisters of the Sacred Heart also have their own language: one built over centuries around mystery, discernment, contemplation, communion, and co-creation with the divine. Their vocabulary is just as rich and just as precise. It describes how knowledge moves, how understanding deepens, and how wisdom serves others.

The insight was simple: these two languages are describing many of the same things.

A neural network, thousands of nodes working in concert where no single node holds the answer but together they form something greater than themselves? That's the communion of saints. Deep learning, the capacity to move beyond surface patterns into layered, profound understanding? That's contemplative prayer. A large language model, a system trained on vast human knowledge to make wisdom accessible and communicable? In the beginning was the Word.

I wrote the whole thing out before the meeting. Every major AI concept, mapped to the theological framework they've spent their lives studying. Not as a gimmick or a trick, but because the parallels are real. Generative AI is co-creation. A pre-trained model is a novice in formation. ChatGPT is the ministry in action, where all that preparation finally meets the people who need it.

The Presentation

When I stood up to present, I looked at nobody but them.

Not the CFO, not the provost, not the other board members. Just the sisters. I watched their eyes. I explained each concept in their language, and before I moved to the next one, I waited. Not for a question or a comment, just for the nod. A simple movement of the head and a small smile. I understand. Go on.

That was my signal. Every single time.

Deep learning? I watched Sister's face. When she connected it to the interior castle of St. Teresa of Avila, the many mansions of deepening prayer, I saw the recognition. Nod. Transformers? I described the Transfiguration, the breakthrough moment where something hidden is suddenly revealed. Nod. A language model trained on the whole of human text to make knowledge accessible? The Word made flesh. Nod. Smile.

I didn't rush. I didn't skip ahead. Each concept earned its own moment, and each moment was confirmed by the people in the room I trusted most to tell me whether it landed.

And when it was over, the sisters didn't just understand AI. They saw themselves in it. They saw their life's work reflected in a new technology, and that changed the entire conversation from fear to curiosity, from resistance to recognition.

The Real Work

Here's what most people in technology get wrong about AI adoption: they think the hard part is the technology. It isn't. The hard part is the translation.

AI has arrived with a vocabulary that was built by and for engineers. That vocabulary is precise and useful inside the field, and completely alienating outside of it. When we present AI to a boardroom, a faculty senate, a community group, or a congregation and use our native jargon, we're not communicating. We're performing. And performance doesn't build understanding; it builds distance.

Empathetic listening is the opposite of performance. It means understanding your audience's language before you ask them to learn yours. It means knowing that a sister who has spent sixty years in religious life has a framework for understanding knowledge, wisdom, formation, and service that is not inferior to ours. It is, in fact, deeper. She just needs a bridge between her framework and the new one, and building that bridge is our job.

This isn't about dumbing things down. The sisters are brilliant, every one of them, with decades of intellectual rigor behind their faith. This is about respecting the architecture of understanding that already exists in someone's mind and connecting new knowledge to it. That's not simplification; it's good teaching. And if we're honest, it's exactly what the best AI systems do: take vast complexity and make it accessible in the language the user already speaks.

Inclusion Is Greater Than Exclusion

AI is here. It has its own language. And right now, that language is keeping most of the world on the outside of a conversation that will shape everyone's future.

Translating AI into the language of the Sacred Heart wasn't a one-time parlor trick. It was a proof of concept for something much bigger. Every community has its own vocabulary, its own frameworks, its own way of understanding how the world works. Farmers, nurses, artists, veterans, teachers, tradespeople. Each of them will be profoundly affected by AI, and none of them should have to earn a degree in computer science to participate in deciding how.

Inclusion is greater than exclusion. That's not a slogan; it's a strategy. The future of AI will be shaped by whoever shows up to shape it, and if the only people who show up are the ones who already speak the language, then the future will be built for a very small audience. The rest of the world will have AI done to them instead of with them.

Everyone in technology right now has a job that goes beyond building things. The job is translation. Finding the language that makes this real for the people in front of you, whether they're sisters in a boardroom, teachers in a break room, or patients in a waiting room. Watching their faces. Waiting for the nod.

And when you get it, you'll know something that no technical demo or slide deck can tell you: they're with you. Not because you dazzled them with jargon, but because you met them where they are, in the language they already trust, and gave them a reason to believe this future includes them.

That nod from a Sister of the Sacred Heart meant more to me than any standing ovation from a tech conference ever has. Because it meant the message actually landed. And if it can land there, it can land anywhere.

You just have to care enough to translate.
